# Titan-Encoder-Based Temporal Point Process for Intermittent Demand Forecasting

## Abstract
Intermittent demand consists of long zero-demand periods interrupted by irregular positive events with highly variable magnitudes. Rather than treating this setting as fixed-interval regression, we formulate it as prediction over a marked temporal event sequence. For each future demand event, the model predicts a demand-size class, an inter-event time, and a continuous residual used to reconstruct the final order quantity. This magnitude-factorized design preserves the interpretability of demand classes while reducing the information loss caused by assigning each class a single representative value.

We first apply this formulation to recurrent marked temporal point process models and then combine it with a Titan sequence encoder, yielding TitanTPP. Experiments on an intermittent spare-parts demand dataset and a weekly aggregated yellow-taxi demand dataset show that the proposed formulation improves quantity reconstruction and that TitanTPP achieves the strongest overall validation performance after convergence. On the taxi-demand dataset, TitanTPP reduces quantity MAE by 13.8% (2827.4 to 2436.1) and lowers validation NLL by 11.5% (1.602 to 1.417) relative to the RMTPP baseline. These results suggest that modeling sparse demand as marked event sequences is effective, and that richer sequence encoders are especially beneficial when demand timing and magnitude are highly heterogeneous.

## Introduction
Forecasting intermittent demand is difficult because the signal is sparse in time and highly uneven in scale. In many practical settings, such as spare-parts inventory or localized transportation demand, long stretches of zero demand are interrupted by positive orders whose arrival times and quantities vary substantially. Standard fixed-interval forecasting models treat every calendar bucket as an equally informative regression target, but this view often spends most of its capacity modeling zeros while compressing the more important question: when will the next positive demand event occur, and how large will it be?

Temporal point processes provide a more natural lens for this setting. Instead of predicting a dense sequence of periodic quantities, they model the conditional distribution of the next event given the event history. For intermittent demand, however, a useful point-process model must represent more than event timing. It must also predict demand magnitude in a way that is both learnable and meaningful for downstream quantity reconstruction. A naive marked point-process formulation can assign each event to a quantity bin, but reconstructing the final demand from a single representative value per bin discards within-bin variation and makes the result sensitive to bin boundaries.

This study addresses the problem by formulating intermittent demand forecasting as a marked temporal event prediction task with a magnitude-factorized quantity representation. Each positive demand event is represented by a coarse log-magnitude mark and a continuous residual within that magnitude class. The final demand estimate is reconstructed as `quantity = base^(mark + residual)`. This design keeps the mark prediction interpretable while allowing the model to recover continuous order quantities without relying on one fixed representative value for each class.

We first instantiate this formulation with recurrent marked temporal point process models, then extend it with a Titan sequence encoder. The resulting model, TitanTPP, preserves the temporal point-process prediction heads for mark, inter-event time, and value residual, while replacing the recurrent backbone with a richer sequence encoder designed to capture longer and more heterogeneous event histories. This separation lets us study two questions independently: whether magnitude-factorized event modeling is useful for intermittent demand, and whether a stronger sequence encoder improves next-event prediction under the same output formulation.

A second issue is the alignment between the training objective and the final quantity metric. In the basic formulation, the value head is supervised in residual space, while quantity MAE is computed only after exponential reconstruction. This can create an objective-metric mismatch because small log-scale errors may become large quantity errors after rescaling. We therefore also examine direct quantity supervision and hybrid loss variants that combine residual learning with a direct reconstructed-quantity objective.

Our experiments evaluate the proposed formulation on an intermittent spare-parts demand dataset and a weekly aggregated yellow-taxi demand dataset. The results show that TitanTPP achieves stronger overall validation performance than the RMTPP baseline after sufficient training, with especially clear gains on the taxi-demand dataset where demand magnitudes are more heterogeneous. The quantity-supervision ablation further suggests that direct quantity objectives are most valuable when scale variation is large, while hybrid supervision offers a practical balance between quantity accuracy, residual fidelity, and training stability.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. We reformulate intermittent demand forecasting as marked temporal event prediction over positive-demand sequences rather than dense fixed-interval regression.
2. We introduce a magnitude-factorized representation that decomposes demand quantity into a coarse log-magnitude mark and a continuous residual for final quantity reconstruction.
3. We develop TitanTPP, a Titan-encoder-based temporal point process model that uses the same mark, time, and residual heads as recurrent TPP baselines.
4. We empirically compare RMTPP and TitanTPP across two demand datasets and analyze how direct quantity supervision affects reconstruction accuracy and validation performance.
