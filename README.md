# Working Paper Draft

## Abstract

Forecasting intermittent and highly variable demand remains challenging because observations are sparse, event timings are irregular, and demand magnitude can span multiple orders of magnitude. To address these issues, we formulate intermittent demand forecasting as a marked temporal point process problem and replace coarse representative-quantity decoding with a magnitude-factorized prediction scheme. In the proposed formulation, the next event is modeled through three coupled targets: an order-class mark, an inter-event time, and a residual value term used to reconstruct continuous demand quantity. This design preserves the interpretability of magnitude classes while reducing the information loss introduced by fixed bin representatives. We implement this formulation on top of recurrent marked temporal point processes and extend it with a Titan-based sequence encoder, yielding TitanTPP. Experiments on two datasets, an intermittent spare-parts demand dataset and a weekly aggregated yellow-taxi demand dataset, show that TitanTPP achieves the best overall validation score after sufficient training, while also improving quantity reconstruction quality on both datasets. The gains are especially clear on the more complex taxi-demand setting, where TitanTPP substantially reduces quantity error and improves validation likelihood compared with the RMTPP baseline. These results suggest that magnitude-factorized temporal point process modeling is effective for sparse-demand forecasting and that richer sequence encoders become particularly beneficial when demand dynamics are more heterogeneous and volatile.
